### Q1 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: C — *effective*

**Passage summary:** The ancient Inca used terraced farming with built-in irrigation systems at multiple altitudes, producing a "great number and variety of crops."

**Why C is correct:** "Effective" means successful at producing a desired result. The positive outcome (abundant, varied crops) shows the method worked — it was effective.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Positive/Negative Charge Match
> The passage tone is positive: the method "produced positive results." The blank needs a positive word that means "it worked well." Eliminate negative or neutral words first.

**Why others fail:**
- **A (chaotic):** The passage describes a *systematic* approach (deliberately constructed terraces, built-in irrigation) — the opposite of chaotic.
- **B (uniform):** The method produced a *variety* of crops — the opposite of uniform sameness.
- **D (burdensome):** The text emphasizes advantages, not difficulties.

### Q2 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: C — *relying on*

**Passage summary:** Astronauts have regularly repaired the Hubble Space Telescope, but this is expensive. Robots might soon take over these repairs.

**Why C is correct:** "Relying on" means depending on. The passage says the ongoing *involvement* of astronauts — the need to *depend on* them repeatedly — is what's expensive. Robots would eliminate that dependency.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Who/What is the Subject of the Expensive Action?
> The sentence structure reveals the logic: "because _______ astronauts is expensive." The blank describes *what you do with* astronauts that's costly — you *depend on* them.

**Why others fail:**
- **A (straightening):** Makes no sense — you can't "straighten" astronauts.
- **B (forgetting):** The passage says nothing about forgetting astronauts.
- **D (reducing):** "Reducing" in status is unrelated to cost.

### Q3 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: C — *neglect*

**Passage summary:** Election polling has often failed at predicting winners. But neuroscientist Sam Wang says campaigns shouldn't _______ polling, because polls are still valuable for campaign strategy even when they fail to predict outcomes.

**Why C is correct:** "Neglect" means to disregard or pay insufficient attention to. Wang's point: don't throw out polling just because it sometimes fails to predict winners — it still has strategic value.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: The "Even Though" Logic
> The passage structure is: "Polling fails at X BUT is still useful for Y → don't _______ it." The blank must mean "dismiss/disregard."

**Why others fail:**
- **A (distort):** Wang isn't saying campaigns shouldn't give false accounts — he's saying don't abandon polling.
- **B (enact):** Polling already exists; "enact" (establish for the first time) doesn't fit.
- **D (supplement):** Wang's point is about whether to use polling at all, not about adding to it.

### Q4 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: D — *integral*

**Passage summary:** Steiger Butte Drum collaborated with composer Michael Gordon on *Natural History* AND "must be included in all performances."

**Why D is correct:** "Integral" means essential or necessary to the whole. Both the creation and the performance require Steiger Butte Drum — they are not optional; they are essential.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Necessity Language
> Key phrase: "must be included in all performances." "Must" signals necessity → "integral" (essential). If something *must* happen, it's integral.

**Why others fail:**
- **A (tangential):** Means peripheral or barely relevant — the opposite. The ensemble is central.
- **B (subsequent):** Means following after. The ensemble was involved from the beginning, not just later.
- **C (analogous):** Means comparable to something else. No comparison is being made.

### Q5 · Words in Context (Literary) · ✅ Answer: B — *manifest*

**Passage summary:** The sun had already set below the horizon, but its presence was still _______ in the "tongues of fire" (beams of light) flickering in the darkening sky.

**Why B is correct:** "Manifest" here means *perceptible* or *detectable*. The sun itself was invisible (below the horizon), but its presence could still be *detected* through the remaining light.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Literary Vocabulary — What Does It Mean HERE?
> "Manifest" can mean several things. In context: the sun isn't visible, yet its presence *shows itself* through the remaining light. "Perceptible/detectable" captures this meaning precisely.

> [!danger] Trap: "Situated" (C) or "Realized" (A)
> "Realized" means brought into being — the sun already exists. "Situated" means located in — the sun isn't *located inside* the light. Focus on the idea of *perceived through* or *detectable via*.

**Why B is correct:** The text follows a clear narrative arc: (1) the elimination occurred, (2) bad consequences followed, (3) the wolf was reintroduced to fix those consequences. B accurately captures this cause-and-consequence-then-solution structure.

> Before looking at answer choices, outline the passage: What happens first? What follows? What's the outcome? Then find the answer that matches your outline.

**Why A fails:** The text presents *one* solution actually implemented (reintroduction in 1996), not multiple potential solutions to choose from.

### Q13 · Information & Ideas (Data) · ✅ Answer: C — *The percentage of veterans in both houses decreased by more than 50 percentage points between 1973 and 2013.*

![[assets/q13_veterans_congress.png]]

**Key data points:**
- **1973:** House ~74%, Senate ~79%
- **2013:** House ~17%, Senate ~18%
- **Drop:** ~57 percentage points (House), ~61 percentage points (Senate) — both well over 50 points

**Why C is correct:** The graph shows a dramatic, consistent decline from 1973 to 2013 of more than 50 percentage points in both chambers. C accurately describes this as the study's key finding.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Calculate the Change Explicitly
> For "percent change" questions: Identify the two years and subtract. 74 − 17 = 57 points. 79 − 18 = 61 points. Both exceed 50 → "more than 50 percentage points" is accurate.

### Q15 · Information & Ideas · ✅ Answer: C — *Earthworms from surface soils constitute a major part of olms' diet.*

**Passage summary:** Scientists previously believed olms stayed in underwater caves their whole lives. Manenti's team *claims* olms regularly surface to find food. The question asks what finding would *support* this claim.

**Why C is correct:** Earthworms live in surface soil — they would NOT be found in underwater caves. If olms regularly eat earthworms, the only way to get them is to *surface*. This directly supports the claim that olms leave their caves to find food.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: "Strengthen the Claim" = Find What Makes the Claim More Likely True
> The claim: olms go to the surface to eat. Supporting evidence must be something that *would only make sense if olms really do surface*. Earthworms are exclusively surface-dwellers → perfect evidence.

> [!danger] Trap: Answer A (breeding frequency)
> Information about how often olms breed tells us nothing about whether they surface to *eat*. The claim is specifically about finding food — stay focused on that.

### Q16 · Information & Ideas · ✅ Answer: C

**Claim:** Stevens strove to convey the *ephemeral and dynamic* characteristics of ikaah (Navajo sandpainting practice).

### Q17 · Information & Ideas · ✅ Answer: A — *Enhance their reputations by appearing especially brave and strong*

**Passage summary:** Edelweiss became symbols of courage in Switzerland. Mountain climbers spread the false idea that edelweiss grew only in dangerous, hard-to-reach places. Historian Scheidegger says these claims were "self-interested."

**Why A is correct:** "Self-interested" = serving one's own purposes. If climbers said edelweiss grew only in dangerous places, then anyone who found edelweiss would seem brave. The climbers were building their own *reputations* for courage and strength.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: "Self-interested" = What Did They Personally Gain?
> Always ask: what do the climbers *get* by making this false claim? If they claim only the brave can reach it, then reaching it proves you're brave → reputation boost.

**Why B fails:** Making the flowers seem harder to reach would *discourage* tourism, not encourage it. And the text doesn't suggest tourism promotion was their motive.

### Q18 · Information & Ideas · ✅ Answer: A — *Some discrepancies between the model's simulations and Titan's actual precipitation and humidity*

**Passage summary:** The IPSL model of Titan's atmosphere uses a simplified, *uniform* methane mole fraction for its lowest atmospheric level. But real methane distribution across Titan's atmosphere is almost certainly *not* uniform. Since methane mole fraction affects precipitation and humidity, the simplified model would logically produce some inaccuracies in those outputs.

**Why A is correct:** Uniform assumption ≠ actual non-uniform distribution → the model's precipitation and humidity outputs would diverge from Titan's reality. This is a logical cause-and-effect conclusion.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Simplification → Inaccuracy in Outputs
> Any model that *simplifies* a complex input (real methane distribution) will produce *simplified / slightly inaccurate outputs* for things that depend on that input (precipitation, humidity). The model's simplification is the cause; output discrepancies are the effect.

**Why C fails:** C says the actual methane mole fraction must be *higher* than the model's value. The text gives no basis for this direction — it only says the real distribution is non-uniform, not whether it's higher or lower.

### Q19 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *why ganga has been used as a communication method.*

**Sentence:** "Anthropologists have investigated _______"

Full correct form: "Anthropologists have investigated **why ganga has been used as a communication method.**"

**Why B is correct:** This completes the sentence with an **indirect question** — a "wh-" clause embedded inside a declarative sentence. Indirect questions use **declarative word order** (subject + verb: *ganga has been*) and the sentence ends with a **period**.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Indirect vs. Direct Questions
> - **Direct question:** "Why *has ganga been* used?" (inverted, question mark)
> - **Indirect question:** "...why *ganga has been* used." (normal order, period)
> The whole sentence is a *statement about what anthropologists studied*, not a question.

> [!danger] Trap: "has ganga been" (A) — inverted order
> Inverted subject-verb order (has + subject) is the direct question form. Inside a declarative sentence, this is ungrammatical.

### Q20 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *to create*

**Sentence:** "ArcGIS facilitates the creation of maps by analyzing and arranging geographic and demographic data **to create** visual representations..."

**Why B is correct:** The **to-infinitive** ("to create") correctly forms a subordinate clause expressing *purpose* — ArcGIS analyzes and arranges data *in order to create* visual representations.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: To-Infinitive for Purpose
> When a clause explains the *purpose* of an action (why something is done), use the **to-infinitive** (to + base verb). "ArcGIS analyzes data **to create** maps" = it does X *in order to achieve* Y.

**Why others fail:**
- **A (create):** Bare infinitive without "to" creates an ungrammatical sentence here.
- **C (creating):** A present participle can't form this kind of purpose subordinate clause.
- **D (created):** Past tense finite verb creates an ungrammatical, run-on structure.

### Q21 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *[no punctuation]*

**Sentence:** "The part of a compound that determines the compound's color is called **the chromophore**."

**Why B is correct:** No punctuation is needed between the verb phrase "is called" and its **complement** "the chromophore." The complement directly completes the verb's meaning and must not be separated from it.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Verb + Complement = No Punctuation
> Verb complements are necessary to complete the verb's meaning. Never separate a verb from its complement with punctuation:
> ❌ "is called, the chromophore"
> ❌ "is called: the chromophore"
> ✅ "is called the chromophore"

### Q22 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *cooperation—*

**Full sentence:** "The solution to the solar panel alignment challenge—**assuming solar panel installers' cooperation**—that was proposed by the city council..."

**Why B is correct:** The supplementary element "assuming solar panel installers' cooperation" is bracketed by **two dashes** (one after "cooperation" to match the one already placed after "challenge"). Dash pairs correctly offset this interrupting parenthetical.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Matched Punctuation — Dash Must Pair with Dash
> When one side of a parenthetical uses a dash, the other side must also use a dash. Mixing dash + comma or dash + colon is always wrong.
> **Rule:** comma–comma OR dash–dash, never mixed.

### Q23 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *newts, for instance,*

**Full sentence:** "In a JSON file storing data about fire belly **newts, for instance,** you might use..."

**Why B is correct:** "For instance" is a **supplementary parenthetical element** that signals an example. It needs to be set off with commas on both sides: *newts**,** for instance**,** you might use...*

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Transitional Phrases Need Comma Pairs
> Phrases like *for instance*, *for example*, *however*, *therefore* when inserted mid-sentence need commas on both sides.

**Why A fails:** Removing the comma after "newts" creates a sentence fragment: "In a JSON file storing data about fire belly newts" — the following "for instance" has nothing to attach to properly.

### Q24 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *Amorphous ice, defined by a disorganized molecular structure, forms...*

**Opening modifier:** "Defined by a disorganized molecular structure, _______"

**Why B is correct:** The modifier "defined by a disorganized molecular structure" must be followed immediately by **amorphous ice** — the thing that actually has a disorganized molecular structure. B places "amorphous ice" as the subject right after the comma, creating a clear, logical connection.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Modifier Placement — The Subject Must Match the Description
> Ask: what is "defined by a disorganized molecular structure"? The answer is *amorphous ice*. That noun must come immediately after the comma.

**Why A fails:** A places "ice Ih" after the modifier — but ice Ih has a *hexagonal* (organized) structure, not a disorganized one. This creates a factual contradiction AND a dangling modifier.

### Q25 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: A — *Electrograms, recordings of electrical activity in the brain, were...*

**Opening modifier:** "Recordings of electrical activity in the brain, _______"

**Why A is correct:** What are "recordings of electrical activity in the brain"? **Electrograms** — that's their definition. A places "electrograms" as the subject right after the modifier, correctly matching the description.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: "Recordings of X" = Thing Being Defined
> A modifier that defines what something IS must be immediately followed by the thing it defines. "Recordings of electrical activity in the brain" = electrograms → electrograms must come first.

**Why B fails:** B places "the most highly skilled soccer players" after the modifier — players are not "recordings of electrical activity." Classic dangling modifier error.

### Q26 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *searching*

**Sentence:** "Scientists who adapted the mirror test for snakes, **searching** for evidence of self-awareness, found that..."

**Why B is correct:** The **present participle** "searching" correctly forms a **supplementary participial phrase** that modifies "scientists." It describes what the scientists were doing while they adapted the test — a simultaneous ongoing action.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Participial Phrases Modify the Subject
> A participial phrase (-ing or -ed form) placed after a comma that follows a noun modifies that noun. "Scientists... *searching* for evidence" = scientists who were looking for evidence.
> This is different from a finite verb clause, which would need a conjunction.

**Why A fails (searched):** "Searched" is a finite past-tense verb. Using it here without a conjunction creates an ungrammatical run-on sentence.

### Q27 · Transitions · ✅ Answer: D — *Finally,*

**Structure (Turkish paper marbling process):**
- "First": Fill a tray with a liquid solution
- "Next": Add inks or paints to the solution (creating a design)
- Blank sentence: The artisan slips paper in and out of the liquid to transfer the design

**Why D is correct:** "Finally" signals the **last step** in a sequence. After "first" and "next," the paper transfer is the concluding step. "Finally" correctly marks this as the end of the process.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Sequence Transitions Follow the Set ("First… Next… Finally")
> When a passage uses explicit sequence markers (first, next, then), the final step takes a conclusion marker: *finally*, *last*, *ultimately*.

### Q28 · Transitions · ✅ Answer: B — *Instead,*

**Structure:**
- Previous sentence: Researchers *hypothesized* jorō spiders would be aggressive (given their large size).
- Blank sentence: When disturbed, the spiders "freeze" in place — they are actually gentle.

**Why B is correct:** The discovery *contradicts* the initial hypothesis. "Instead" signals that what follows replaces or contradicts what came before — perfect for when a finding is the *opposite* of the prediction.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Hypothesis vs. Discovery
> When researchers *expect* X but *find* Y, use a contradiction transition: *instead*, *however*, *but*, *on the contrary*.

**Why A (therefore) fails:** "Therefore" signals the discovery is a *result of* the hypothesis. But a hypothesis isn't a cause — it's a prediction. The finding doesn't *follow from* the hypothesis; it *contradicts* it.

### Q29 · Transitions · ✅ Answer: C — *Indeed,*

**Structure:**
- Previous sentence: Jets reaching the ionosphere (~80 km) are *outliers*.
- Blank sentence: The majority of jets reach only 20–50 km.

**Why C is correct:** "Indeed" signals **emphasis or reinforcement** of the preceding claim. The fact that most jets reach only 20–50 km *confirms and reinforces* that jets reaching 80 km are exceptional outliers.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: "Indeed" = "And in fact, here's the proof"
> "Indeed" adds emphasis or provides confirming evidence for the previous claim. It's not a contrast or result — it's a strengthening. "Jets reaching 80 km are outliers. *Indeed*, most jets only reach 20–50 km."

**Why A (nevertheless) fails:** "Nevertheless" would signal a contrast — as if the rest of the sentence *goes against* the outlier claim. But the low typical height *supports* the outlier claim, not contrasts it.

### Q30 · Transitions · ✅ Answer: B — *Ultimately,*

**Structure:**
- Previous sentence: Dillard *struggled to reconcile* the juxtapositions of nature (beautiful yet brutal).
- Blank sentence: For Dillard, nature's intricacy and harshness are **inextricably linked**.

**Why B is correct:** "Ultimately" signals that what follows is the **final conclusion or realization** reached after a process (her struggle to reconcile contradictions). This is the endpoint of her intellectual journey.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Process → Conclusion
> When a passage describes a struggle or process and then delivers the end result, use a conclusion transition: *ultimately*, *finally*, *in the end*, *at last*.

## 🔑 Master Strategy Summary

> [!abstract] Top Strategies for Module 2
>
> **Words in Context (Q1–5)**
> Look for *definition clues* within the passage (phrases like "that is," "meaning," or restatements). Match the blank's *charge* (positive/negative/neutral) to the context. In literary passages, ask what the word does *in that specific moment*, not what it usually means.
>
> **Craft & Structure (Q6–9)**
> For text-structure questions: outline the passage in 3–4 bullet points first, then find the answer that matches your outline. For function questions: ask "What job does this sentence/phrase perform relative to what came before or after?"
>
> **Information & Ideas (Q10–18)**
> For data questions: anchor to the *specific year or category* the question asks about. Calculate changes numerically when asked about "more/less" or percentage differences. For "strengthen a claim" questions: the evidence must address the *specific* aspect of the claim being tested — avoid evidence that is only loosely related.
>
> **Standard English Conventions (Q19–26)**
> - **Indirect questions** (Q19): declarative word order + period
> - **To-infinitive for purpose** (Q20): "to + verb" expresses why
> - **Verb + complement: no punctuation** (Q21)
> - **Matched punctuation** (Q22–23): dash pairs with dash, comma pairs with comma — never mix
> - **Modifier placement** (Q24–25): the noun right after the comma must be what the opening phrase describes
> - **Participial phrases** (Q26): use -ing (present participle) to describe simultaneous action
>
> **Transitions (Q27–30)**
> Identify the logical relationship between sentences FIRST:
> - Sequence final step → *finally*
> - Contradiction of hypothesis → *instead*, *however*
> - Emphasis/reinforcement → *indeed*
> - Final conclusion after a process → *ultimately*
>
> **Rhetorical Synthesis (Q31–33)**
> Match the answer to the student's SPECIFIC goal. The goal is your filter: check that (1) the answer addresses exactly what the goal asks, (2) all facts are accurately represented from the notes, and (3) no information is mixed up between the two subjects being compared.
