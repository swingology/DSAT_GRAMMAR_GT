### Q1 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: A — *comprehend*

**Passage summary:** Ezra Pound's poetry is dense, experimental, and full of obscure references. Many readers have difficulty even identifying the poems' subjects.

**Why A is correct:** "Comprehend" means to understand or grasp meaning. The passage describes poetry so complex that readers can't identify its subject — they struggle to *understand* it. "Hard to comprehend" fits perfectly.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Words in Context
> Read the blank in context and identify the **tone and logic clues**. Here: *dense*, *experimental*, *difficult time identifying subjects* → all point to a comprehension difficulty.

> [!danger] Trap: Overlook (D)
> "Hard to overlook" means easy to notice — the opposite of what the passage implies. The text isn't about visibility; it's about complexity and difficulty understanding.

**Why others fail:**
- **B (dislike):** Text doesn't address likeability — it addresses difficulty of understanding.
- **C (interrupt):** Not discussed. Interrupting reading is not the issue.
- **D (overlook):** Means "fail to notice" → implies very noticeable, contradicts the idea of obscurity.

### Q2 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: C — *interconnected*

**Passage summary:** Bali's subak water management system has cultural and historical significance. Its elements — terraces, canals, water temples — are described as being joined into "a single cohesive unit."

**Why C is correct:** "Interconnected" means linked or joined together — exactly what the phrase "joined together into a single cohesive unit" tells us.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Synonym Lock
> The blank is essentially defined by the very next clause: *"they are joined together into a single cohesive unit."* Look for the word that is a **direct synonym of the definition given in the passage**.

**Why others fail:**
- **A (outmoded):** Means outdated. The text says the opposite — it emphasizes the system's lasting significance.
- **B (informal):** No evidence the system is informal.
- **D (optional):** The passage says the elements work together as one, not that they are optional.

### Q3 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: D — *suppress*

**Passage summary:** The Soviet government tried to _____ Vladimov's novel *Faithful Ruslan*, but copies circulated in secret.

**Why D is correct:** "Suppress" means to forcibly put down or prevent from spreading. The key clue is the contrast: the government *tried* to stop the book, yet it "circulated in secret." A government trying to eliminate a book is suppressing it.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Contrast Clue
> The word "although" signals a contrast. The government's action failed — copies still circulated in secret. So the action must have been a strong attempt to **stop or eliminate** the book.

> [!danger] Trap: Critique (B)
> Governments critiquing art is not suppression. "Critique" has no element of restriction or banning, which is what the contrast implies.

### Q4 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: D — *contradicted*

**Passage summary:** Scholars thought silk spread from China in the 2nd century CE. This view has been _____ by new archaeological evidence showing the Indus Civilization used silk 1,000 years earlier.

**Why D is correct:** New evidence proving a prior belief wrong *contradicts* it. The old view said 2nd century CE; evidence shows 1,000 years earlier — this directly contradicts the previous belief.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Logical Relationship
> Ask: What does new evidence *do* to an old scholarly belief? If it proves the old belief wrong, it **contradicts** or **overturns** it.

**Why others fail:**
- **A (investigated):** Evidence investigates a claim; it doesn't describe the relationship between new and old conclusions.
- **B (misinterpreted):** The scholarly view is not just misread; it's wrong.
- **C (anticipated):** The old view was not predicting the new evidence — it was wrong about it.

### Q5 · Words in Context · ✅ Answer: A — *unassuming*

**Passage summary:** A casual description of García's mural can make it seem _____ — it's in a coffee shop and shows women drinking coffee — but in fact it's complex and demands serious attention.

**Why A is correct:** "Unassuming" means modest or not impressive at first glance. The passage says a *casual description* makes the work sound trivial — that's unassuming. The contrast word "but in fact" confirms the first impression is understated.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Contrast Word ("but")
> "But in fact" signals the blank describes a *wrong first impression*. The work seems modest/trivial but is actually complex and important. "Unassuming" = appears modest.

> [!danger] Trap: Pretentious (D)
> "Pretentious" means claiming more importance than deserved — the opposite! The work actually IS important; it just doesn't *appear* that way.

### Q9 · Craft & Structure · ✅ Answer: D — *By contending that the kinds of reactions controversial public artworks often receive aren't exclusively the result of attributes inherent in the works themselves*

**Passage:** Text 1 says public art that challenges norms almost inevitably provokes backlash. Text 2's critics note that norm-defying art not effectively integrated into its surroundings tends to be viewed more unfavorably than similarly provocative museum art.

**Why D is correct:** Text 1 says backlash is caused by the works' inherent qualities (unconventional appearance/ideas). Text 2's critics would counter that context matters — the *same* art in a museum gets a *better* response. So the reaction isn't just about the work itself; it's also about placement/context. The critics would say reactions aren't *exclusively* about the work's inherent attributes.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Cross-Text — Identify the CLAIM in Text 1 and what Text 2 would SAY ABOUT it.
> Text 1's underlined claim: backlash is caused by the art's unconventional nature. Text 2 adds: context (placement) matters too. So the critics modify Text 1's claim by adding a contextual factor.

> [!danger] Trap: Answer A
> A says the public's disagreement is *unrelated* to the art's unconventionality. But Text 2's critics don't fully dismiss unconventionality — they just say it's not the ONLY factor. "Not exclusively" (D) is more precise than "unrelated" (A).

### Q14 · Information & Ideas · ✅ Answer: D — *The study by McAllister et al. suggests that when traveling waves intersect in specific ways, the resulting wave may be higher than would be expected based on the properties of traveling waves.*

**Passage:** Traveling waves break when steepness exceeds a threshold — this limits their height. McAllister et al. studied *spike waves* (axisymmetric, formed when opposing traveling waves meet). Spike waves can exceed the height constraint because additional factors (jet stability, cavity shape) mediate their breaking.

**Why D is correct:** The main idea is that spike waves (formed when traveling waves intersect in specific ways) can be *taller than expected* based on what we know about traveling waves alone — because different factors control their breaking.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Main Idea — The "so what?" of the whole passage.
> Ask: What does the study ADD to our knowledge? Answer: Waves formed by intersecting traveling waves can be taller than the normal height limit for traveling waves.

> [!danger] Trap: Answer A
> A incorrectly says McAllister found that jet stability and cavity shape influence *traveling wave* breaking. The text says these factors influence *spike wave* breaking — not traveling waves.

### Q19 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: D — *you should choose.*

**Sentence:** "Click restraint is the practice of scanning a search results page and evaluating what you see before deciding which link _______"

**Why D is correct:** The blank completes an **indirect question** embedded in a declarative sentence. Indirect questions (starting with "which," "what," "whether") are NOT questions — they're embedded clauses. They use **declarative word order** (subject + verb) and end with a **period**, not a question mark.

Correct form: "which link **you should choose**." (subject: you, verb: should choose)

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Direct vs. Indirect Questions
> - **Direct question:** "Which link *should you choose*?" (inverted order, question mark)
> - **Indirect question:** "...deciding which link *you should choose*." (normal order, period)
> The entire sentence is a *statement* about click restraint, not a question.

> [!danger] Trap: Answers A and C (should you choose)
> These use inverted word order ("should you") — correct for direct questions but WRONG for embedded indirect questions.

### Q20 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: D — *had become*

**Sentence:** "By 2023, she _______ one of the most celebrated musicians in Latin America, having released twelve albums..."

**Why D is correct:** **Past perfect tense** ("had become") is used when one past action was completed **before another past reference point**. "By 2023" = a past reference point. Lafourcade's status as celebrated musician was achieved before/up to 2023. Past perfect = **had + past participle**.

$$\text{Past Perfect} = \textit{had} + \text{past participle}$$

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Verb Tense — Timeline Clues
> "By 2023" = the action was completed at some point up to 2023. In a past-narrated sentence, use **past perfect** (had become) to show completion before a past reference point.

| Tense | Form | When to use |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Simple Past | became | completed at a specific past time |
| Past Perfect | **had become** | completed before another past reference point |
| Future | will become | happening after now |
| Future Perfect | will have become | will be completed before a future point |

### Q21 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: A — *includes*

**Sentence:** "Currently, the frog's range _______ parts of northern New South Wales and southeastern Queensland."

**Why A is correct:** The word **"currently"** is a present-tense signal. The simple present "includes" correctly describes the frog's *current* geographic range.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Time Adverbs as Tense Anchors
> Words like *currently*, *now*, *today*, *at present* → **simple present** tense. Words like *formerly*, *once*, *previously* → **simple past** or **past perfect**.

### Q22 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *1804,*

**Sentence:** "The 12th amendment, ratified in _______ separated the elections for the two offices."

**Full structure:** "The 12th amendment, ratified in 1804**,** separated..."

**Why B is correct:** The phrase "ratified in 1804" is a **supplementary (nonessential) element** — it provides extra info about *which* amendment but is not essential to the sentence's meaning. Nonessential elements are set off with **commas on both sides**: *amendment**,** ratified in 1804**,** separated*.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Comma Pairs for Nonessential Phrases
> If you can remove the phrase and the sentence still makes grammatical sense and retains its core meaning, it's nonessential → use commas.
> **Test:** "The 12th amendment separated the elections." ✅ Still makes sense → the middle phrase is nonessential → needs commas.

> [!danger] Trap: Dash (A)
> A dash cannot be paired with a comma. Both sides of a nonessential phrase must use the *same* punctuation: comma–comma OR dash–dash.

### Q23 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: B — *the titles allow the listener to approach each piece free from expectations.*

**Sentence opens with:** "Laden with emojis and seemingly meaningless words, and driven largely by Sawano's 'personal feeling and mood,' _______"

**Why B is correct:** The opening modifying phrases describe **the titles** (of Sawano's musical scores). The subject immediately following the comma must be what's being modified. Only B places **"the titles"** as the subject, correctly linking the modifier to what it describes.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Dangling Modifier Rule
> The noun immediately after the opening modifier clause must be the thing being described.
> "Laden with emojis... the **titles** allow..." ✅
> "Laden with emojis... the **listener** can approach..." ❌ (Listeners aren't laden with emojis!)

### Q24 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: A — *explore*

**Sentence:** "Barrada's pieces, utilizing elements as disparate as plant-dyed fabrics, wire crab traps filled with stones, and cotton balls dangling above a fan, _______ the ways humans attempt to organize and regulate nature."

**Why A is correct:** Subject = "Barrada's **pieces**" (plural). The verb must agree in number → plural verb "**explore**."

> Long interruptive phrases ("utilizing elements as disparate as...") can obscure the subject. Cross out the middle phrase mentally:
> "Barrada's pieces _______ the ways..." → plural subject → plural verb **explore**.

### Q25 · Standard English Conventions · ✅ Answer: D — *swimming,*

**Full sentence with answer:** "Helical **swimming,** widely understood to confer stability and efficiency in the locomotion of a variety of microscopic organisms—including bacteria, eukaryotic algae, and ciliates—bestows similar advantages, albeit via different propulsive modes, to larger oceanic macroplanktons, such as salps."

**Why D is correct:** The long dash-bounded phrase ("widely understood...ciliates") is a **supplementary element** interrupting the main clause. The main clause is: "Helical swimming **[—...—]** bestows similar advantages..." A comma after "swimming" pairs with the opening dash to correctly offset the interrupting element.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Mixed Punctuation Offsetting
> When an interrupting element is set off by a dash on one side, and there's already a comma from the list inside, the correct opener may be a comma before the dash-bounded phrase. Look at what **makes the main clause readable**.

### Q26 · Transitions · ✅ Answer: D — *As a result,*

### Q29 · Transitions · ✅ Answer: A — *though,*

![[assets/q29_roche_limit.png]]

**Structure:**
- Sentence 1: Debris outside the Roche limit *should* accrete into a satellite (not remain a ring).
- Sentence 2: Morgado et al. detected a ring at 2,500 miles (well *outside* the 1,100-mile Roche limit) that **remained intact**.

**Why A is correct:** This is an **exception** to the expected rule. The ring exists well outside the Roche limit but *hasn't* accreted — contradicting the expectation. "Though" correctly signals this concessive/exception relationship.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Exception/Concession Transitions
> Words like *though*, *however*, *yet*, *despite*, *although* signal that what follows **goes against** what was just stated.

> [!danger] Trap: "Fittingly" (C) or "Likewise" (D)
> These suggest the discovery *aligns with* the expected rule. But it *contradicts* it — the ring is intact where it shouldn't be.

**The Roche Limit concept:**

$$r_{Roche} \approx R_{primary} \left(2\frac{M_{primary}}{M_{secondary}}\right)^{1/3}$$

> [!info] Quick Science Note
> Inside the Roche limit: tidal forces from the central body prevent debris from clumping → rings form and persist.
> Outside the Roche limit: self-gravity dominates → debris accretes into moons.
> Quaoar's ring at 2,500 miles defies this model, suggesting other factors matter too.

### Q30 · Rhetorical Synthesis · ✅ Answer: C

**Goal:** Indicate *how long* John Cage's musical piece will last.

**Notes include:** 639 years, longest continuous musical performance in history.

**Why C is correct:** "Lasting 639 years, John Cage's musical piece will be the longest continuous musical performance in history." — This sentence directly states the duration (639 years) and adds context (longest ever). It's the most **effective synthesis** of the duration-focused goal.

> [!tip] Key Strategy: Match Answer to the SPECIFIC Goal
> The student's goal is specifically about *how long* — not about who composed it, not where it's played, just the duration. C is the only sentence that puts duration front and center.

**Why others fail:**
- **A:** Identifies composer, not duration.
- **B:** Identifies location, not duration.

## 🔑 Master Strategy Summary

> [!abstract] Top Strategies for This Module
>
> **Words in Context (Q1–5)**
> Read around the blank for *context clues* — contrast words ("but"), definitions embedded in the passage, and tone signals. Never pick a word just because it sounds sophisticated.
>
> **Craft & Structure (Q6–9, 12–13)**
> For "function" questions: ask what the sentence *does*, not what it *says*. For cross-text: identify Text 2's position relative to Text 1's specific claim.
>
> **Information & Ideas (Q10–11, 14–18)**
> For inference: stay close to the text. For main idea: find the "so what?" of the whole passage. For data/graphs: read axis labels carefully.
>
> **Standard English Conventions (Q19–25)**
> - Indirect questions → declarative word order + period (Q19)
> - Tense: follow time-signal words (Q20–21)
> - Nonessential phrases → matched punctuation on both sides (Q22, Q25)
> - Modifiers → subject after comma must be what's described (Q23)
> - Subject-verb agreement → find real subject, ignore interruptive phrases (Q24)
>
> **Transitions (Q26–29)**
> Identify the logical relationship between sentences FIRST: Cause/Effect → *consequently, as a result* | Contrast → *by contrast, however* | Exception → *though, despite* | Example → *for example*
>
> **Rhetorical Synthesis (Q30–33)**
> Match the answer to the student's SPECIFIC goal. Check all facts against the notes — wrong answers often *misrepresent* information from the notes.
