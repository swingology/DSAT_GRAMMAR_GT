### Q1 — **D) similarities with**
The sentence says tenrecs and true hedgehogs share traits they "independently developed." The blank needs a phrase meaning *sharing features with*. "Similarities with" is the only idiomatically correct and logically precise option. "Examples of" and "indications of" don't work grammatically with the structure; "concerns about" is off-topic.

**Skill:** Vocabulary in Context / Idiomatic Precision

---

### Q2 — **B) an overtly**
The contrast is key: 1920s–1960s stories had politics that were "less deliberately signaled," so the 1970s mode must be the opposite — openly, explicitly feminist. "Overtly" (openly/blatantly) captures this contrast. "Prudently" (cautiously) and "inadvertently" (accidentally) contradict the logic; "cordially" (pleasantly) is irrelevant.

**Skill:** Vocabulary in Context / Contrast Logic

---

### Q3 — **B) Redressing**
Scholars "began to expand their focus" away from the old overemphasis — they are *correcting* an imbalance. "Redressing" means remedying a wrong or imbalance. "Exacerbating" means worsening; "epitomizing" means exemplifying; "precluding" means preventing — all contradict the meaning.

**Skill:** Vocabulary in Context / Action Verbs

---

### Q4 — **D) Providing resistance to**
The vendors and shoppers are physically blocking the taxi's path with their wagons and handtrucks. "Disputing" here means *contesting or resisting* passage — not arguing verbally. "Providing resistance to" best captures this physical obstruction. "Arguing about" and "disapproving of" imply verbal objection; "asserting possession of" doesn't fit the passage scenario.

**Skill:** Vocabulary in Context — Literary Passage

---

### Q5 — **D) inconsequential to**
The sentence structure is: scholarship undermined claims that Ibn Rushd was **[blank]** other Muslim philosophers — but his geographic isolation is still undeniable. The undermined claim must be that he *didn't matter* to other philosophers; scholars now recognize he did have influence. "Inconsequential to" fits. The geographic detail supports that his isolation made influence difficult, but scholars say he still mattered.

**Skill:** Vocabulary in Context / Complex Sentence Logic

### Q6 — **A) It elaborates on the previous sentence's statement about a transitional moment**
The first sentence says Johnson's style underwent an "abrupt transformation." The underlined sentence (turning from expressionist landscapes to bold portraits of Black Americans) *elaborates* on that transformation — it tells us specifically what changed and how. It doesn't call Johnson's development into question (D), and it's not about his travels or personal life (B, C).

**Skill:** Text Structure & Purpose — Function of a Sentence

---

### Q7 — **B) To assert that Argerich's performances look effortless because of how she prepares**
The text's core argument: Argerich *appears* spontaneous, but this is actually the result of intensive preparation. Option B captures this cause-and-effect claim directly. A and D are too narrow (the text never lists specific pieces she chooses or her first-time encounters); C misreads the passage entirely.

**Skill:** Central Idea / Author's Main Purpose

---

### Q8 — **B) It suggests that if not for a certain aspect of his character, Israel might not have been as easily thwarted**
The underlined phrase — "singular patience and mildness" — explains why Israel accepted the betrayal rather than fighting back. B correctly reads this as a character trait that made him easier to thwart. A is wrong (Israel doesn't "treasure" it); C and D misread the trait as a physical limitation or an explanation for courage.

**Skill:** Text Structure — Function of an Underlined Portion

---

### Q9 — **D) By suggesting dance companies should consider revised versions instead of completely rejecting the show**
Text 1 says The Nutcracker "should no longer be produced." Text 2 agrees it's outdated but argues it should be *kept and updated* (citing Hot Chocolate Nutcracker). Text 2's author would push back against full rejection, advocating for revision instead. A, B, and C all misrepresent Text 2's position.

**Skill:** Cross-Text Connections / Paired Passages

### Q10 — **D) Medina-Alcaide and her team tested Paleolithic light sources and learned some details about how Paleolithic artists traveled within dark caves**
The passage describes a study that tested three lighting methods to understand cave navigation. D accurately summarizes the main idea without overstating (A says fireplaces were "essential" — not stated; B says lamps were used *more often* — the opposite; C says they were reluctant to conclude — also wrong).

**Skill:** Central Idea / Main Purpose

### Q11 — **A) The number of cars produced increased but the number of companies producing cars decreased**
From 1910 to 1925: cars went from 123,990 → 3,185,881 (massive increase); companies went from 320 → 80 (large decrease). A is the only option that correctly reflects both trends. The other options are factually wrong per the table.

**Skill:** Quantitative Data — Tables

---

### Q14 — **C) In both the second and third test administrations, participants who took 2–4 days of leave had higher average attentiveness scores than did participants who took 1–5 weeks of leave**
The researchers concluded that *longer* leave doesn't confer *greater* benefit than shorter leave. C directly shows 2–4 day leave outperforming 1–5 week leave in both post-leave tests — supporting the conclusion. A and D don't compare the two leave groups against each other; B addresses the first (pre-leave) test, which is irrelevant to the conclusion.

**Skill:** Data Analysis — Graphs / Supporting a Conclusion

---

### Q15 — **B) Multiple lizard species move at an average of less than 90% of their maximal speed while escaping predation**
The text argues that even while *escaping*, animals don't always use maximum speed. The graph shows lizard species in the 30–39% through 80–89% escape ranges, confirming that multiple species use less than 90% even while fleeing. This is the best evidence for the claim. A misreads the comparison; C overstates the 90–100% bar; D is about pursuing, not escaping.

**Skill:** Data Analysis — Graphs / Evidence for a Claim

### Q12 — **A) "I needed to buy some cleaning supplies, but they weren't in their regular place..."**
The researchers explain that rearranged layouts cause shoppers to move through more of the store and encounter unplanned items. Response A perfectly illustrates this: the shopper searched for a displaced item and made an unplanned purchase (the notebook). B shows a failed search; C involves a coupon (an explicit cue, not the layout effect); D involves store size, not layout changes.

**Skill:** Command of Evidence — Textual

---

### Q13 — **A) Between them, the exhibitions included drawings, paintings, photographs, sculptures, textiles, videos, costumes, and music**
The underlined claim is that the exhibitions "showcase the diverse **media** through which artists have depicted" the Black experience. Only A directly addresses *media* (types of artistic formats). B and D address number of artists and time span; C addresses source of works — none of these concern media types.

**Skill:** Command of Evidence — Supporting a Specific Claim

---

### Q16 — **D) Analysis of water's molecular structure under high pressure reveals that hydrogen bonds are more stable when TMAO is present**
The hypothesis: TMAO reduces water's compressibility by stabilizing hydrogen bonds. D shows TMAO directly stabilizing hydrogen bonds under pressure — the most direct support. A contradicts the hypothesis (TMAO would be ineffective); B is about TMAO's own structure, not water's; C shows a *positive* correlation between TMAO and compression rate, which would contradict the hypothesis.

**Skill:** Evaluate a Hypothesis / Scientific Reasoning

---

### Q17 — **D) Flew for shorter distances and spent more time walking than researchers previously thought**
Beccari's team found T. navigans had traits making sustained flight *difficult* and upright walking *comfortable* — the opposite of what was previously assumed (powered flight as primary locomotion). D logically follows. A contradicts the findings; B and C aren't supported by the evidence presented.

**Skill:** Inference / Logical Completion — Scientific Passage

---

### Q18 — **B) Ethical consumers in their twenties are less likely to purchase the phone than ethical consumers in other age groups**
The theory: purchase likelihood *positively correlates* with perception of a product's effects. Twentysomethings rated the phone's effects *less positively* → they should be *less likely* to buy it. B is the direct logical implication. A involves a comparison to other phones (not in the study); C says there's no meaningful difference (contradicts the data); D reverses the reasoning.

**Skill:** Inference / Logical Completion — Social Science Passage

### Q19 — **A) Created**
The blank introduces a participial phrase modifying "the saloon": *Created by businessman William A.G. Brown, the saloon was known to offer...* The past participle "Created" is needed. "Creates" and "Create" are finite verbs (would create a run-on); "Creating" would imply present action and is awkward here.

**Skill:** Participial Phrases / Modifiers

---

### Q20 — **C) popularized**
"Her innovative poems popularized the use of Jamaican Creole in literature." The sentence is a clean subject-verb-object construction needing no punctuation after "popularized." A semicolon (A) would require an independent clause after it; a comma (B) creates a comma splice or unnecessary break; a colon (D) requires a list or explanation to follow.

**Skill:** Punctuation — Avoiding Unnecessary Marks

---

### Q21 — **D) creates**
"...antimetabole, a writing technique that **creates** emphasis by repeating a statement in a reversed order." The subject of the verb is the singular "technique," requiring the singular "creates." "Create" (A) is plural; "are creating" (B) and "have created" (C) are incorrect tense/aspect for describing a technique's general function.

**Skill:** Subject-Verb Agreement

---

### Q22 — **D) people perceive acts of kindness.**
"Researchers...investigated how **people perceive acts of kindness.**" This is an *indirect question* (embedded within the sentence as a noun clause) — it ends with a period, not a question mark. Options A and B use inverted question word order (do they/do people), which is only correct for *direct* questions. C incorrectly adds a question mark.

**Skill:** Direct vs. Indirect Questions / End Punctuation

---

### Q23 — **D) reveal**
"Such quotidian objects...painted by Vermeer..._______ the daily minutiae..." The subject is the plural "objects," so the verb must be plural: "reveal." "Was revealing" (A) and "has revealed" (B) are singular/wrong tense; "reveals" (C) is singular.

**Skill:** Subject-Verb Agreement (with interrupting phrase)

---

### Q24 — **C) microsculptures. Creations**
The sentence needs a boundary after "microsculptures" because the next part ("so small that they are best viewed through a microscope") is a separate, complete modifying clause. A period creates two proper sentences. "Microsculptures creations" (A) is a noun pile-up; the comma (B) creates a comma splice; "and" (D) creates a run-on with a mismatched conjunction.

**Skill:** Sentence Boundary Punctuation / Run-ons

---

### Q25 — **B) works. Because**
"A ray diagram reveals how this **works. Because** the hole's small size restricts light..." A period correctly ends the first complete thought. "Because" then begins the second sentence explaining *why* it works. "Works because" (A) creates a fragment; "works, it's because" (C) is awkward and redundant; "works: it's because" (D) — the colon is possible but "it's" is redundant after it.

**Skill:** Sentence Boundary / Subordinating Conjunctions

### Q26 — **D) Instead,**
The scientists propose *avoiding* "life" — and then propose using "lyfe" as an *alternative*. "Instead" signals substitution/replacement. "Previously" is temporal (wrong context); "Regardless" means despite (wrong logic); "There" is locative (nonsensical here).

**Skill:** Transitions — Alternative / Contrast

---

### Q27 — **D) Today,**
The sentence contrasts the past state (5% display before 2009) with the current state (30% display). "Today" is the logical temporal transition. "Additionally" adds information rather than contrasting past/present; "For example" signals an illustration; "Nevertheless" signals unexpected contrast between two similar things.

**Skill:** Transitions — Temporal / Contrast

---

### Q28 — **C) on the other hand,**
Lower elevations: snowpack deepest in early March. Higher elevations: snowpack deepest in mid-March. These are *contrasting* findings, so "on the other hand" is correct. "In other words" restates; "for instance" introduces an example; "in summary" concludes.

**Skill:** Transitions — Contrast

---

### Q29 — **C) In addition,**
The Inca used quipus for countable data (established), AND they may have also used them for stories/myths (new information being added). "In addition" signals supplemental information. "As a result" implies causation; "In other words" restates; "For example" would require the second fact to be an example of the first.

**Skill:** Transitions — Addition

---

### Q30 — **B) then,**
"It may not have been a coincidence, **then**, that Darwin and Wallace arrived at the concept independently." Here "then" functions as "therefore" — drawing a logical conclusion from the preceding observation (that natural selection was an "obvious next step"). "However" contradicts; "moreover" adds; "for example" would introduce an illustration.

**Skill:** Transitions — Logical Inference ("then" = therefore)

### Q31 — **A) By crossbreeding...Ammal succeeded in creating sugarcane hybrids well suited to India's climate**
The goal is to emphasize *Ammal's achievement*. A names her, describes her specific method, and states the successful result — centering her accomplishment. B centers the institution; C is vague; D uses passive voice and omits her name from the key action.

**Skill:** Rhetorical Synthesis — Emphasizing a Person's Achievement

---

### Q32 — **B) Recognition and Students Aspire both show African American figures in poses that symbolize supportive relationships**
The goal is to emphasize a *similarity*. B explicitly states what both sculptures share: African American figures in poses symbolizing supportive relationships. A only discusses Students Aspire; C only gives dates; D highlights a *difference* (rounded vs. defined features).

**Skill:** Rhetorical Synthesis — Identifying Similarities

---

### Q33 — **C) Although most materials used in dhow replicas are traditional, some modern materials are used**
The goal is a *generalization about materials*. C makes a broad claim about the mix of materials (mostly traditional, some modern) — exactly what a generalization should do. A focuses only on one specific material (coconut palm rope); B is about the ancient dhow itself; D is about the shipbuilders' activity, not a generalization about materials.

**Skill:** Rhetorical Synthesis — Making a Generalization
