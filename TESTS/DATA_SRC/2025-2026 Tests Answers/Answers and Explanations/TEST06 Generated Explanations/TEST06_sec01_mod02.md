# SAT Practice Test 6 — Reading & Writing: Module 2
### Answer Key with Explanations

## Questions 1–5 — Words in Context

**Q1 — Answer: C) featured**
Hernandez's works are now displayed *in museums*, which contrasts with her earlier outdoor murals. "Featured" fits because art is *featured* in museum exhibitions. "Invented" and "recommended" don't fit; "adjusted" is wrong contextually.
> 🎯 *Skill: Words in Context — Vocabulary precision*

---

**Q2 — Answer: D) inspired by**
The sentence says Garza draws from memories and surroundings — she is fueled by direct experience. "Inspired by" captures that creative source. "Complimented by," "uncertain about," and "unbothered by" all fail logically.
> 🎯 *Skill: Words in Context — Logical word choice*

---

**Q3 — Answer: C) accidental**
Skeptical colleagues doubted the kea's tool use was deliberate — they thought it was *accidental*. Bastos then proved it was intentional. "Intriguing" and "observable" don't contrast with "deliberate"; "obvious" doesn't fit either.
> 🎯 *Skill: Words in Context — Contrast logic*

---

**Q4 — Answer: B) manifest in**
Historians argue Morris's rejection of industrialization *shows up in* (is manifest in) his use of handmade materials and medieval-style ornamentation. "Insensible to" and "complicated by" reverse or distort the logic.
> 🎯 *Skill: Words in Context — Idiomatic precision*

---

**Q5 — Answer: B) tentative**
Pennington's place in memory is *less certain* than Kwolek's (who invented Kevlar and "will long be remembered"). "Tentative" means uncertain/not firmly established — the right contrast. "Permanent" and "prominent" are opposites; "warranted" doesn't fit.
> 🎯 *Skill: Words in Context — Comparative logic*

## Questions 6–12 — Comprehension & Analysis

**Q6 — Answer: C) To portray Francie's determination to meet a goal**
The passage shows Francie methodically reading every book alphabetically, not skipping dry ones, persisting even when it's "hard going." This is about determination, not enjoyment of a topic or preference for reading over other activities.
> 🎯 *Skill: Main Purpose of a Text*

---

**Q7 — Answer: D) To describe an experiment whose results cast doubt on an established hypothesis**
The text presents the established hypothesis (Clovis point hunters drove mammoths extinct), then an experiment (spears penetrating only a few inches into clay), then a conclusion that contradicts the hypothesis. Classic "experiment challenges hypothesis" structure.
> 🎯 *Skill: Main Purpose / Text Structure*

---

**Q8 — Answer: C) It concedes that not all people in medieval Europe had access to public bathhouses**
The underlined clause "While such amenities were available mainly to town dwellers" acknowledges a limitation — not everyone had access — before pivoting to note that bathing was still common via rivers and sponge baths. It's a concession, not a limitation of prior historians' work.
> 🎯 *Skill: Function of a Sentence/Clause*

---

**Q9 — Answer: C) It describes a common approach to studying the Chicano movement that, according to the text, obscures the ideological diversity of the movement's participants**
The underlined portion explains that scholars focus on militant figures, making the movement *seem* uniformly radical — which then Herrera corrects by revealing diverse ideologies. The problem is the scholarly approach obscures diversity.
> 🎯 *Skill: Function of a Sentence/Clause*

---

**Q10 — Answer: C) Although one might expect that foreign investment would increase as natural-resource extraction makes up a larger share of developing countries' economies, the opposite happens because heavy reliance on natural resources can lead to unattractive conditions for investors**
Asiedu found a *negative* correlation — the counterintuitive finding. The text explains the reason: boom-bust cycles destabilize currencies and increase uncertainty. Choice C captures both the surprise and the explanation. Choice A misidentifies the reason.
> 🎯 *Skill: Main Idea / Synthesis*

---

**Q11 — Answer: C) It ruled out a potential alternative explanation for the acceleration in microorganism-mediated nutrient cycling**
If community composition had *changed*, researchers couldn't attribute the faster cycling to temperature alone (it could be different microorganisms). The unchanged composition eliminates that alternative explanation, isolating temperature as the cause.
> 🎯 *Skill: Inference / Scientific Reasoning*

---

**Q12 — Answer: C) It should be treated as a biosignature gas if detected in the atmosphere of a rocky planet but not if detected in the atmosphere of a mini-Neptune**
On rocky planets, NH₃ can't reach high levels without life → it's a valid biosignature there. On mini-Neptunes, atmospheric pressure/temperature can produce NH₃ without life → not a valid biosignature there. Choice C correctly captures this distinction.
> 🎯 *Skill: Inference / Scientific Reasoning*

## Questions 13–18 — Data Analysis & Claims

**Q13 — Answer: C) Yellowstone National Park**
Reading the graph: Yellowstone's line sits highest in all three months (June, July, August). The triangle-marker line is consistently at the top (~800–1,000+ thousand). Rocky Mountain peaks second in some months but never tops Yellowstone in all three.
> 🎯 *Skill: Data Analysis — Graph Interpretation*

---

**Q14 — Answer: D) "I heard a thousand blended notes… pleasant thoughts / Bring sad thoughts to the mind"**
The prompt asks for *contradictory feelings* — pleasant and sad simultaneously. Choice D explicitly shows this tension ("pleasant thoughts / Bring sad thoughts"). The other choices describe pleasant nature scenes without the emotional contradiction.
> 🎯 *Skill: Textual Evidence / Illustrating a Claim*

---

**Q15 — Answer: D) were higher for all three measures, indicating greater overall well-being for these participants**
Checking the table: Placebo-aware group scored 5.0 vs 3.9 (Global), 92.00 vs 46.00 (Symptom severity), and 11.4 vs 5.4 (Quality of life) — all three are higher. Choice D is the only one that accurately reflects all three data points.
> 🎯 *Skill: Data Analysis — Table Interpretation*

---

**Q16 — Answer: B) NFM attitude has a strong negative effect on political knowledge and interest, and there is known to be a strong positive correlation between political knowledge and interest and the likelihood of voting**
The researchers posit that NFM *indirectly* reduces voting probability. For this to work, NFM must lower political knowledge/interest, AND knowledge/interest must link to voting. Choice B establishes both links of this chain. Choice D doesn't establish that NFM lowers knowledge.
> 🎯 *Skill: Evaluating Evidence / Supporting a Hypothesis*

---

**Q17 — Answer: A) logging may be useful for maintaining healthy forests, provided it is limited**
The study found limited-logging plots *more robust* than unlogged plots. The logical conclusion is that *limited* logging can benefit forest health. Choices B and D contradict the findings; Choice C says conclusions are difficult — but the text already draws a conclusion.
> 🎯 *Skill: Logical Completion / Inference*

---

**Q18 — Answer: D) there is no way to reasonably evaluate how a work would be different if it had been written by other means**
The passage says each novel results from "a specific set of circumstances." Therefore, speculating about alternate conditions is impossible — you can't know what the novel *would have been*. Choice D follows directly from the "unique entity" premise.
> 🎯 *Skill: Logical Completion / Rhetorical Reasoning*

## Questions 19–26 — Grammar & Standard English Conventions

**Q19 — Answer: C) do they affect our weather?**
The sentence poses two questions linked by "and how." Both must be questions, so question word order (do/does + subject + verb) and a question mark are needed. "do they affect our weather?" is correct inverted-question syntax.
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Questions & Sentence Structure*

---

**Q20 — Answer: A) Matthew Henson made several treks across Greenland between 1891 and 1909**
The opening modifier "One of the few African American global explorers during the turn of the 20th century" must modify a *person*, and that person must be the subject of the main clause. Only A places Henson (the person) as the subject immediately after the modifier.
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Dangling/Misplaced Modifiers*

---

**Q21 — Answer: A) the topological tapestries of Argentine textile artist Alexandra Kehayoglou are**
The opening modifier "Woven from recycled yarn and hand tufted…" must describe the *tapestries* (the things that were woven), not the artist herself. Only A makes the tapestries the subject of the main clause that follows the modifier.
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Dangling/Misplaced Modifiers*

---

**Q22 — Answer: C) through;**
Two independent clauses are being joined: "Clear glass…allows all light to pass through" and "wax paper…allows only some light to pass through." A semicolon correctly joins two independent clauses. A comma alone (A) creates a comma splice; "through and" (D) would need rewording.
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Punctuation (Semicolons)*

---

**Q23 — Answer: B) leaves; man-made trash,**
The sentence uses a three-item list with internal commas (each item has a clarifying "such as" phrase), so semicolons separate the items to avoid ambiguity. The structure: "natural debris, such as dried **leaves;** man-made trash**,** such as plastic bags**;** and more traditional art supplies, such as tree glue."
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Punctuation (Semicolons in lists)*

---

**Q24 — Answer: A) competitions, however:**
"However" is a transitional word interrupting the main clause and must be set off by commas. After listing the five dances, a colon introduces them. The correct structure is: "competitions**,** however**:** rumba, samba…"
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Punctuation (Colons & Commas)*

---

**Q25 — Answer: D) indiscriminate:**
The word "indiscriminate" completes the subject complement, and the colon introduces the explanation/elaboration that follows ("they will devour all kinds of shrubs…"). A colon is correct when what follows explains what came before. A comma (B) is too weak; the others don't work grammatically.
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Punctuation (Colons)*

---

**Q26 — Answer: B) increase**
The subject is "the toxins" (plural), so the verb must be plural: "increase" (not "increases" or "is increasing"). The sentence structure: "the toxins…actually **increase** its attractiveness." Plural subject → plural verb.
> 🎯 *Skill: Grammar — Subject-Verb Agreement*

## Questions 27–33 — Transitions & Rhetorical Synthesis

**Q27 — Answer: D) Finally,**
The paragraph describes a sequence of steps in an archaeological dig: survey → dig → record artifacts → then removal and lab analysis. "Finally" correctly signals the last step in a process. "For instance," "On the contrary," and "Earlier" don't fit a sequential final step.
> 🎯 *Skill: Transitions — Sequence*

---

**Q28 — Answer: A) in turn,**
Liquid metals circulate → generate electrical currents → **in turn** → those currents create a protective barrier. "In turn" shows a chain of cause-and-effect. "Likewise" implies similarity; "nevertheless" implies contrast; "in reality" implies correction.
> 🎯 *Skill: Transitions — Cause & Effect*

---

**Q29 — Answer: B) in fact,**
The sentence says the differences between the 1881 and 1908 editions are "extreme, even by James's standards" — and then adds that some critics view them as two different novels. "In fact" intensifies or elaborates on an extreme claim. "By contrast" would signal opposition; "nevertheless" signals concession; "in other words" signals restatement.
> 🎯 *Skill: Transitions — Elaboration/Intensification*

---

**Q30 — Answer: C) Both the Choctaw Code Talkers and the Navajo Code Talkers transmitted coded military messages in the soldiers' native languages**
The task is to emphasize a *similarity*. Choice C explicitly compares both groups using "Both" and highlights the shared method (transmitting coded messages in their native languages). Choices A and B describe only one group; D highlights a difference.
> 🎯 *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis — Emphasizing Similarity*

---

**Q31 — Answer: D) While there have been only about 1,200 recorded meteorite falls, there have been over 60,000 meteorite finds**
The task is to *contrast* the numbers. Choice D does this directly with "While…only…there have been over…" — placing both numbers in direct opposition. The other choices define the terms or give only one figure.
> 🎯 *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis — Contrast*

---

**Q32 — Answer: B) Among the many botanical samples Doña María do Carmo Bandeira collected was Polytrichum juniperinum, a species of moss she collected from Serra de Itatiaia in 1925**
The task is to emphasize the *Serra de Itatiaia* sample specifically. Choice B foregrounds the Polytrichum sample from that location while still crediting Bandeira's broader work. Choice C mentions both samples equally; D mentions both without the location detail; A doesn't mention the sample at all.
> 🎯 *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis — Emphasis*

---

**Q33 — Answer: C) Having the potential to damage national security if disclosed, most routine diplomatic correspondence is classified as Confidential**
The task is to indicate which *category* routine diplomatic correspondence belongs in. The notes say it could cause "damage" (not "serious damage"), and "damage" = Confidential. Choice C directly connects the category name (Confidential) to the rationale. Choice A is convoluted; B doesn't name the category; D doesn't either.
> 🎯 *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis — Category/Classification*
