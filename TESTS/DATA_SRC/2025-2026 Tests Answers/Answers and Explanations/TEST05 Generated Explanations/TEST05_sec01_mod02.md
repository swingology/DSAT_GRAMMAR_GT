# SAT Practice Test 5 — Reading & Writing Module 2
## Answer Key with Explanations

## Vocabulary in Context

### Q1 — **C) Stretching toward**
> *Skill: Words in Context (Phrase)*

Mrs. Wilkins is physically extending her arm across the table toward the bowl of oranges — "stretching toward" captures that literal, physical action. *Joining with* implies connection; *gaining on* implies catching up; *arriving at* implies completion — none match a mid-motion reach.

---

### Q2 — **D) Obtain**
> *Skill: Words in Context*

The team was able to *get* detailed information from the fossils. *Obtain* = acquire/get. *Occupy* means to take up space; *hoard* means to stockpile selfishly; *reserve* means to set aside — none fit extracting information from fossil data.

---

### Q3 — **A) Reduced**
> *Skill: Words in Context*

The passage sets up a contrast: more decisive when information flow is *intensified* vs. slower when it is _______. The logical antonym of intensified in this context is *reduced*. The other choices don't form a meaningful contrast with "intensified."

---

### Q4 — **A) A tenuous**
> *Skill: Words in Context*

The War of 1812 has a weak, fragile hold on British historical memory — it is overshadowed by the Napoleonic Wars and changed nothing for Britain geopolitically. *Tenuous* = weak/slight. *Enduring* and *conspicuous* are opposites of the intended meaning; *contentious* (disputed) isn't supported.

---

### Q5 — **C) Defend**
> *Skill: Words in Context*

Claims about Minoan bull-leaping are hard to *successfully defend* because so little is known and any argument inevitably rests on speculation. *Defend* fits the context of justifying or proving a claim. *Imagine* and *summarize* don't require evidence; *adjust* implies modification, not argumentation.

## Function of a Sentence / Rhetorical Purpose

### Q6 — **C) It emphasizes that Harris always boasts about his own constitution when speaking of a previous boat trip**
> *Skill: Function of a Sentence*

The underlined sentence is the comedic punchline of an escalating pattern: first it was Harris and the captain, then Harris and the second mate, then Harris and one other man, then Harris alone. The humor lies in Harris always positioning himself as the one person not ill — revealing his self-aggrandizing tendency. Choice D is wrong because nothing suggests he prefers solitary travel.

---

### Q7 — **B) It identifies a conflict between research findings and recent events that the text goes on to suggest is a consequence of a complicating factor in the data used to generate those findings**
> *Skill: Function of Underlined Portion*

The underlined sentence establishes the tension: studies show negligible electoral consequences for austerity, yet some European governments suffered. The rest of the passage explains this conflict is due to governments strategically timing austerity to hide its costs — a complicating factor that corrupted the study data. Choice A is wrong because the text doesn't say the studies lacked real-world data; Choice D mischaracterizes what follows.

---

### Q8 — **A) By asserting that language plays a more important role in graphic novels than the author of Text 1 recognizes**
> *Skill: Cross-Text Connections*

Text 1 argues graphic novels use language "only sparingly." Text 2 directly counters this: without captions and dialogue, illustrations are incomprehensible — language is essential. Text 2 would push back by saying Text 1 underestimates the role of language. Choices B, C, and D would involve agreeing with Text 1, which Text 2 clearly does not.

---

### Q9 — **A) Lutie is observing the appearance of the street at a particular time of day and the events occurring on it**
> *Skill: Literal Comprehension / What is Happening*

Lutie watches the sunset-lit street and notices children playing games. The passage is purely observational and appreciative — there's no annoyance (B), puzzlement (C), or isolation (D) expressed anywhere in the text.

---

### Q10 — **D) It is unclear whether Bosco Verticale can support the plant life included in its design**
> *Skill: Explicit Information*

The text states critics note the trees "were specifically cultivated for the project" but "it's too early to tell if they can thrive in this unusual setting." Choice D restates this concern accurately. Choices A, B, and C introduce information not found in the passage.

---

### Q11 — **A) Literary theorist Mikhail Bakhtin argued that there are important characteristics of narratives that are not fully encompassed by two concepts that other theorists have used to analyze narratives**
> *Skill: Main Idea*

The passage introduces fabula and syuzhet, uses *The Godfather Part II* as an example, then pivots to Bakhtin's view that these two concepts alone are insufficient. The main idea is Bakhtin's critique of those concepts as incomplete. Choice B overstates Bakhtin's point; choices C and D focus on secondary details.

## Command of Evidence — Quantitative

### Q12 — **D) The highest number of medicine and health topic submissions during the period shown is approximately 285 in 2019**
> *Skill: Graph Analysis / Supporting a Claim*

The claim is that medicine and health submissions were highest in 2019. To *support* this claim, you need to show the 2019 peak value directly. Choice D does exactly that. Choice B actually *contradicts* the claim (saying physics exceeded medicine in 2019); choices A and C discuss irrelevant topics.

---

### Q13 — **D) "Through interbreeding, transgenic fish might introduce the trait of fluorescence into wild fish populations, making those populations more vulnerable to predators"**
> *Skill: Supporting Quotation / Evidence*

The student's claim is that the escape of transgenic fish "may have significant *negative long-term ecological effects*." Only Choice D describes an actual ecological harm mechanism — fluorescence spreading to wild fish, making them easier for predators to find. Choice A is just an observation; B acknowledges insufficient data; C is about the scope of introduction, not harm.

---

### Q14 — **C) Growth with nitrogen under moderate change exceeded growth without nitrogen under moderate change, but the latter exceeded growth without nitrogen under extreme change**
> *Skill: Bar Graph Analysis*

The conclusion is that nitrogen can *offset* climate change's negative effects under *moderate* but not *extreme* change. From the graph: "with nitrogen / moderate change" (~0.19) > "without nitrogen / moderate change" (~0.15) > "without nitrogen / extreme change" (~0.04). This shows nitrogen helps at moderate change but cannot overcome extreme change — matching the conclusion perfectly. Choice D is the trap: it inverts the current/moderate comparison.

---

### Q15 — **D) "Reading [poetry], however, with a perfect contempt for it, one discovers that there is in / it after all, a place for the genuine"**
> *Skill: Illustrative Quotation*

The claim is *ambivalence* — acknowledging poetry's merits while also expressing displeasure. Choice D captures both feelings in a single line: contempt (displeasure) alongside the discovery of genuine value (merits). Choices A and B are purely positive; Choice C expresses frustration only, not both sides.

---

### Q16 — **B) If baseline precipitation is somewhat concentrated, water use for irrigation will increase only slightly, whereas it will increase 9.0% for surface water and 7.9% for groundwater if baseline precipitation is evenly distributed**
> *Skill: Table Analysis*

The conclusion is that the change in irrigation is *highly sensitive to baseline concentration*. The table shows a dramatic contrast: small irrigation increases (0.4%, 0.9%) when precipitation is already concentrated vs. large increases (9.0%, 7.9%) when it is evenly distributed. Choice B highlights this sensitivity directly. Choice A mixes in the aquifer statistic; Choice D incorrectly claims irrigation will *decline*.

## Inference / Logical Completion

### Q17 — **A) The English dialects spoken in the Upland South acquired their emphasis on the "r" sound from dialects spoken in Scotland**
> *Skill: Inferences / Logical Completion*

Scottish settlers colonized the Upland South → both regions share the same "r" emphasis → logical conclusion: Upland South dialects inherited this feature from Scottish English. Choice C reverses the direction of influence; B is speculative; D contradicts what the evidence shows.

## Standard English Conventions

### Q18 — **A) Published**
> *Skill: Verb Form / Tense*

The sentence needs a finite past-tense verb to serve as the main predicate: "Nery and her colleagues *published* a study." Choices B, C, and D are non-finite forms that can't serve as the main verb of the clause.

---

### Q19 — **B) Instrument**
> *Skill: Punctuation — Unnecessary Punctuation*

"The strings inside the instrument are plucked" is a clean, simple sentence with no natural pause or separation needed between "instrument" and "are." No colon (A), em-dash (C), or comma (D) is warranted — they would all incorrectly interrupt the flow.

---

### Q20 — **A) Them**
> *Skill: Pronoun Reference*

"He considered _______ dangerous and unnecessary luxury items" — the pronoun refers back to "table forks," a plural noun, so the plural pronoun *them* is correct. *This*, *that*, and *it* are singular and don't agree with "forks."

---

### Q21 — **C) Sisters,**
> *Skill: Punctuation — Appositives*

"A fictionalized account of the lives of the Mirabal sisters" is an appositive that renames/describes the novel. The appositive begins with a dash after "Butterflies," so it must close with a comma after "sisters" to properly end the interrupting phrase. Choice A adds "and," which is ungrammatical here; Choice D has no closing punctuation for the appositive.

---

### Q22 — **C) Dislodge, in part due to its sheer size,**
> *Skill: Punctuation — Interrupters / Parenthetical Phrases*

The phrase "in part due to its sheer size" is a parenthetical modifier that needs to be set off by commas on both sides. The sentence then continues with "it's as heavy as…" — note that "it's" here begins a new independent clause connected by implication. Choice C correctly places commas around the interrupting phrase. Choice B incorrectly uses a colon; Choice D breaks up "in part" unnecessarily; Choice A omits necessary punctuation entirely.

---

### Q23 — **D) Character, Tamatoa the crab**
> *Skill: Punctuation — Appositives*

"Tamatoa the crab" is a restrictive appositive identifying *which* character — it needs a comma after "character" to set it off as a clarifying phrase. No colon (C) is appropriate here; Choice A lacks the comma; Choice B adds a trailing comma that severs it from "belongs."

---

### Q24 — **C) Underlie**
> *Skill: Subject-Verb Agreement*

The subject is "the various cultural and ideological frameworks" — a plural noun. The verb must be plural: *underlie*. Choices A and D (*underlies*, *has been underlying*) are singular. The long interrupting parenthetical "(class, gender, political affiliation, etc.)" does not change the plural subject.

---

### Q25 — **C) Serra, intending**
> *Skill: Sentence Structure / Participial Phrases*

The sentence structure is: "Richard Serra, [participial phrase describing his intention], assembles…" The main verb is "assembles." Choice C gives "Serra, intending his installations to make passersby aware…, assembles" — grammatically correct. Choice D (*Serra intends*) creates two finite verbs ("intends" and "assembles") with no coordinating conjunction, making a run-on. Choices A and B are also structurally flawed.

## Transitions

### Q26 — **B) Next,**
> *Skill: Logical Transitions — Sequential*

The passage describes steps in order: first scraped the ice → _______ sprayed water → lastly smoothed. The blank falls in a numbered sequence, so *Next* is the correct sequential transition. *For example* implies illustration; *Similarly* implies comparison; *In contrast* implies opposition — none fit a step-by-step process.

---

### Q27 — **C) Later,**
> *Skill: Logical Transitions — Temporal*

In 2014, Gomez won a competition. _______ in 2017, he created a show. The blank bridges two events in chronological order, so a time-based transition is needed. *Later* fits perfectly. *Instead* implies substitution; *For example* implies illustration; *In other words* implies restatement.

---

### Q28 — **D) For this reason,**
> *Skill: Logical Transitions — Causal*

Scientists need unchanging measurement standards → *therefore* metrologists developed the SI system. The SI is a direct response to that need, making *For this reason* (cause → effect) the correct choice. *In contrast* and *Regardless* imply opposition; *In addition* implies adding a new point rather than explaining a consequence.

---

### Q29 — **D) Accordingly,**
> *Skill: Logical Transitions — Causal*

The lesson of the Human Genome Project is that genes interact with protein products of other genes → *therefore* researchers have begun studying the proteome. The second sentence is a direct consequence of the first. *Accordingly* = "as a result of this." *In other words* would mean restatement; *That said* implies concession; *For example* implies illustration.

## Rhetorical Synthesis (Note-Taking)

### Q30 — **A) In 1037 CE, al-Biruni published his theory that a large landmass existed west of Europe and east of Asia**
> *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis*

The goal is to specify *when* al-Biruni published his theory. Choice A leads with the date (1037 CE) and includes the theory's content. Choice B omits the date entirely; C says nothing about the publication or theory content; D omits the publication date.

---

### Q31 — **D) With a mass of 2.063 solar masses, Sirius A is more massive than the Sun**
> *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis*

The goal is to *emphasize the mass of Sirius A*. Choice D puts Sirius A's specific mass (2.063 solar masses) front and center and contextualizes it meaningfully. Choice A focuses on Proxima Centauri; Choices B focuses on defining solar masses; Choice C focuses on the Sun and Proxima Centauri.

---

### Q32 — **D) In first-class levers, the fulcrum is located between the effort and the load, but in second-class levers, the load is located between the effort and the fulcrum**
> *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis*

The goal is to *contrast* the two lever types. Choice D directly states the distinguishing feature of each in a single parallel sentence using "but." Choice A contrasts effort and load (not lever types); Choice B is vague ("in different locations"); Choice C notes a similarity, not a contrast.

---

### Q33 — **A) P waves and S waves both travel beneath Earth's surface, causing the ground to move**
> *Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis*

The goal is to emphasize a *similarity*. Choice A correctly identifies what both wave types share: they both travel beneath Earth's surface and cause ground movement. Choice B highlights a *difference* (speed); Choice C describes only P waves; Choice D starts with "although" which signals contrast, not similarity.
