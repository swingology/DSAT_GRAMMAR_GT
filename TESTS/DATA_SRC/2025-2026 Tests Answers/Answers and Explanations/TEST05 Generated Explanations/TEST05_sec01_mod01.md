
# SAT Practice Test 5 — Reading & Writing Module 1

## Answer Key with Explanations

---

## Vocabulary in Context

### Q1 — **A) Evidence**

> _Skill: Words in Context_

Fox-Foot wants no sign of their passing to be left behind — "evidence" is the only choice that fits the idea of leaving a detectable mark. _Blemish_ implies a flaw, _amount_ implies quantity, and _sketch_ implies a drawing.

---

### Q2 — **B) Fragile**

> _Skill: Words in Context_

The sentence immediately explains why: the nose is "delicate and sticks out… making it especially easy to break." _Fragile_ directly matches that reasoning. _Recognizable_ is tempting but doesn't explain why noses break off.

---

### Q3 — **B) Impending**

> _Skill: Words in Context_

The corona brightens _before_ a flare occurs — the flare is about to happen, i.e., _impending_. _Antecedent_ means preceding, _innocuous_ means harmless, and _perpetual_ means constant — none fit the "about to occur" meaning.

---

### Q4 — **D) Exploited**

> _Skill: Words in Context_

Saleem's team took advantage of a known metal property to demonstrate something new. _Exploit_ = use strategically. _Hypothesized_ would mean they only guessed; _discounted_ is the opposite; _redefined_ doesn't fit the context.

---

### Q5 — **B) Ameliorate**

> _Skill: Words in Context_

The sentence calls for a word meaning "to improve or fix" a problematic situation. _Ameliorate_ = to make better. _Sanction_ means approve, _rationalize_ means justify, and _postulate_ means assume — none imply fixing.

---

## Rhetorical Purpose / Main Idea

### Q6 — **D) To explain why the narrator thinks she might make the basketball team**

> _Skill: Main Purpose of a Text_

The narrator lists her physical attributes (quick, wiry, tough, can dodge under others' feet) as reasons she has a chance. The passage isn't comparing sports, describing rules, or explaining selection criteria.

---

### Q7 — **B) To explain that Spanish-language newspapers thrived in Texas and especially in El Paso during the late 1800s**

> _Skill: Main Purpose of a Text_

The passage traces the success of Spanish-language papers in Texas, culminating in El Paso's dominance and explaining why. No present-day comparison, inter-city influence, or Mexico popularity argument is made.

---

### Q8 — **C) To indicate why astrobiologists choose to conduct research in the Atacama Desert**

> _Skill: Function of a Sentence_

The underlined sentence — that the desert "closely mirrors the extreme environment on Mars" — directly answers _why_ researchers study there. It doesn't contrast conditions or describe limitations.

---

### Q9 — **B) It explains why it is challenging to influence transit ridership solely by altering characteristics of a transit system**

> _Skill: Function of Underlined Portion_

The underlined claim ("irreducibly contextual dimension") states that context cannot be removed from the equation — reinforcing the opening argument that copying one system feature won't transfer easily to another city.

---

### Q10 — **B) Introduces an unresolved scientific question → hypothesis → observation that conflicts with the hypothesis**

> _Skill: Overall Text Structure_

Setup: unclear how deep land-use effects extend. Hypothesis: effects go beyond 30 cm. Finding: carbon and nitrogen dropped to similarly low levels past 30 cm regardless of land use — _contradicting_ the hypothesis. Not A (no refinement), not D (data doesn't validate).

## Command of Evidence — Textual

### Q11 — **D) All the marsquakes started from the same location on the planet**

> _Skill: Explicit Information_

The text states directly: "This discovery was surprising to scientists" and the discovery was that all marsquakes started from the same location. The other choices aren't supported by the passage.

---

### Q12 — **A) Maya civilization acquired the use of zero from the Olmec civilization**

> _Skill: Explicit Information_

The text says "Maya mathematicians _inherited_ it from the Olmec civilization." _Inherited_ = acquired. The other choices have no textual support.

---

### Q13 — **D) "When [the banker] got home he lay on his bed, but his tears and emotion kept him for hours from sleeping."**

> _Skill: Illustrative Quotation_

The claim is that the banker was _very upset_. Tears and inability to sleep from emotion directly illustrate that. Choice A shows caution, B shows listening, and C shows delight — the opposite of upset.

---

### Q15 — **C) "I'm really getting quite fond of the big room, all but that horrid [wall]paper."**

> _Skill: Illustrative Quotation_

The claim is _mixed feelings_. This quote shows both a positive feeling (fond of the room) and a negative one (hates the wallpaper). Choices A, B, and D each express only one feeling — not mixed.

## Command of Evidence — Quantitative

### Q14 — **B) Saber-toothed cat (_Smilodon_), which became extinct 11,000 years ago**

> _Skill: Data / Table Analysis_

The longer a species has been extinct, the harder it is to find suitable habitat. The saber-toothed cat (11,000 years) is the most long-extinct candidate on the list. Note: Choice C incorrectly claims the woolly mammoth (6,400 years) went extinct _before_ the saber-toothed cat — the table shows the opposite.

---

### Q16 — **B) _L. pertusa_ declined in the Alboran Sea during a period of substantial local decline in oxygenation, but near the Mauritanian coast during a period of little local change in oxygenation**

> _Skill: Graph Analysis_

High Mn/Ca = low oxygenation (inverse relationship). The Alboran Sea (triangle line) shows a dramatic Mn/Ca spike around 9,000 years ago → low oxygenation coincided with the coral's decline. The Mauritanian coast (square line) shows flat, low Mn/Ca throughout → stable oxygenation, no correlation with the coral's decline.

## Inference / Logical Completion

### Q17 — **A) It can't be concluded that the impact that made the crater was connected to the beginning of the Younger Dryas**

> _Skill: Inferences / Logical Completion_

Kjær's team _cannot yet determine the crater's age_. Without knowing when the impact occurred, there's no basis to link it to the Younger Dryas. This is the only conclusion directly supported by that limitation.

---

### Q18 — **B) Has been preserved in certain mammal species because it benefits their immune systems**

> _Skill: Inferences / Logical Completion_

The appendix hasn't disappeared after emerging + species with it have higher lymphoid tissue (immune support) → the logical hypothesis is that it was retained because it has an immune benefit. Choice C goes too far (predicting future spread).

---

### Q19 — **A) The Aztecs would have disputed the idea that morality can be assessed using standards independent of the individual's social circumstances**

> _Skill: Inferences / Logical Completion_

Aztec ethics judge actions by social role and community contribution — inherently context-dependent. This directly opposes the ethicist view that morality is independent of context. Choice B is too narrow (focusing on evaluators, not standards).

## Standard English Conventions

### Q20 — **C) This**

> _Skill: Pronoun Usage_

"This" refers to the singular event (his appointment) — "making _this_ the first time." _These/those_ are plural; _some_ is indefinite and doesn't work here.

---

### Q21 — **A) Used**

> _Skill: Verb Form / Tense_

The sentence needs a simple past tense finite verb. "Land _used_ his technology to invent…" The other choices (to have used, to use, using) create ungrammatical sentence structures without a main verb.

---

### Q22 — **D) Ensures**

> _Skill: Subject-Verb Agreement_

The subject is the gerund phrase "Using one of these noncopyrighted songs" — singular. It takes a singular verb: _ensures_. The plural forms (are ensuring, have ensured, ensure) don't agree.

---

### Q23 — **B) Hands between the two antennas,**

> _Skill: Plurals vs. Apostrophes_

"Hands" is a simple plural (no possession), and "antennas" is a simple plural. No apostrophes are needed. Apostrophes in choices A, C, and D incorrectly mark possession or are simply wrong.

---

### Q24 — **D) Represent**

> _Skill: Subject-Verb Agreement_

The subject is "Such iconic references" (plural noun), so the verb must be plural: _represent_. The long participial phrase ("cited in rapid… procession by the musician… at a dinner table") is a modifier and does not change the subject.

---

### Q25 — **B) Artist**

> _Skill: Punctuation — Appositives_

"Portrait artist Marie-Denise Villers" is a simple noun phrase naming the person. No punctuation is needed between the noun phrase and the proper name that follows. An em-dash (A) or colon (C) would be disruptive; a comma (D) would create a confusing break.

---

### Q26 — **B) Described, from hair to grass to sculptures—**

> _Skill: Punctuation — Em-Dashes_

The sentence opens a parenthetical with an em-dash after "photographs—". The closing em-dash must appear at the end of the parenthetical, after "sculptures," before "to powerful effect." The comma after "described" sets off the list; the em-dash closes the pair.

---

## Transitions

### Q27 — **D) As a result,**

> _Skill: Logical Transitions_

The technique involves smothering the flame → _therefore_ the vessel turns black. Cause-and-effect is clear. _On the contrary_ signals opposition; _For example_ signals illustration; _Previously_ signals time — none fit.

---

### Q28 — **D) Though,**

> _Skill: Logical Transitions_

The sentence concedes that Morton exaggerated, then pivots to acknowledge his genuine contributions. _Though_ signals this concessive contrast ("No one can deny, _though_, that…"). _Therefore_ would imply his contributions follow from the exaggeration, which is illogical.

---

### Q29 — **B) Conversely,**

> _Skill: Logical Transitions_

Two-party systems vs. multi-party systems are direct opposites. _Conversely_ is the correct contrast word. _Subsequently_ implies time; _For instance_ implies example; _In other words_ implies restatement — none fit a direct contrast.

---

### Q30 — **A) That being said,**

> _Skill: Logical Transitions_

The first sentence establishes a true fact (the shell looks external). "That being said" introduces a complication or nuance — here, that the appearance misleads people into thinking it's an exoskeleton. _However_ would work for a simple contrast; "that being said" better captures the "yes, but here's the nuance" relationship.

---

## Rhetorical Synthesis (Note-Taking)

### Q31 — **A) The real author of _Adam Bede_ was Mary Ann Evans, who published the novel using the pseudonym George Eliot**

> _Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis_

The goal is to _identify the real author_. Choice A directly names Mary Ann Evans and connects her to the pseudonym. Choice D omits her name entirely; choices B and C don't identify the real author at all.

---

### Q32 — **C) When the molten salt in a freeze-thaw battery solidifies at room temperature, energy stops flowing and can be stored in the battery**

> _Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis_

The goal is to explain _how_ salt enables energy storage. Choice C captures the exact mechanism: solidification → energy stops → stored. Choices A and B don't explain the storage mechanism; Choice D only mentions retention rate, not the mechanism.

---

### Q33 — **D) Likely to soon become endangered, the California red-legged frog is classified as threatened by the FWS**

> _Skill: Rhetorical Synthesis_

The goal is to state the frog's _specific classification category_. Choice D names the category ("threatened") and gives the qualifying reason (likely to soon become endangered). Choice A is vague; Choice B only says it's on the list; Choice C incorrectly places it in the _endangered_ category.
